Zero Project

Zero is my first attempt at releasing a demo for an ambitious project developed using Unreal Engine. The core idea is to create a horror game where the player can possess and control enemies, and use magic spells as part of stealth-based mechanics. We are currently polishing the visual aspects before making the demo available.

I designed and programmed Zero, handling every aspect of its development. On the programming side, I implemented all core systems — from gameplay mechanics to the save/load system and level management. Most of the project is written in C++, with Blueprints used where fast iteration was beneficial for design purposes.

Team Size: 2 people
Development Time: 4 months
Technology: Unreal / C++ / Blueprints

Enemy Possession and Combat

A system that allows the player to possess and control enemy characters, either through a full-body first-person controller or by viewing the world from their perspective. Each enemy type offers unique interactions with the environment and different combat abilities for engaging other enemies. Enemies can fight among themselves as well as with the player. Dynamic targeting during combat is managed by a custom team system component. Possessing certain enemies can also unlock specific environmental interactions based on their equipped weapon — for example, igniting explosive barrels with a torch or shooting them with a gun, or breaking barriers using a melee weapon.

Possession and team system in action: the player uses possession to control an enemy with a gun and attack another enemy. The team system makes the attacked enemy react and engage in combat.

Enemy AI

The perception system draws inspiration from Unreal Engine’s perception component functionality and logic, but streamlines the system to include only the features required by the specific game design of the project. The perception system and the behavior tree manage the numerous AI states and transitions needed for this stealth action design, such as Passive, Investigating, Seeking, Frozen, Attacking, Alerting the group, and more. Additionally, the behavior tasks of some states can differ depending on whether the AI agent is part of a “team group” or not.

Development Challenges

Building Zero was my first real attempt at a complex project in Unreal Engine and C++, and the possession system proved to be the most challenging part — requiring deep knowledge of player control, AI behavior, and pawn switching.

Designing a team-based combat system where enemies could dynamically react and switch targets added further complexity. This led me to create a modular team system that enhanced both AI behavior and gameplay responsiveness.

The animation and locomotion system was also a key to achieving the visual polish I wanted. I studied Lyra and ALS, tailoring their features to suit my game’s needs and maintain smooth player feedback.

Lessons Learned

This project pushed me to grow quickly as a developer and gave me a deeper understanding of Unreal’s strengths. It also taught me the value of modular design and the importance of balancing polish with iteration speed.

  • Mastered key Unreal systems like pawn control, AI, and input handling
  • Learned how to design flexible and reusable gameplay components
  • Gained experience in balancing code performance and designer-friendly tools
  • Now approach new projects with clearer architecture and prototyping strategies
